Data Source, Cleaning, and Aggregation

This analysis uses open-source data from the Divvy Trip Data portal provided by Lyft, available at
https://divvy-tripdata.s3.amazonaws.com/index.html.

The dataset contains anonymized trip-level information from the Cyclistic bike-share program, covering October 2024 to September 2025.
Each monthly file includes ride details such as rideable_type, started_at, ended_at, start_lat, end_lat, and user type (member_casual).

Before importing into R, all 12 months of data were cleaned and aggregated using Power Query in Excel.
The cleaning process involved:

  • Removing duplicate and incomplete records (e.g., missing station names or coordinates).
  • Calculating ride duration from started_at and ended_at timestamps.
  • Extracting temporal features such as month, day of week, and hour.
  • Creating ride length buckets (e.g., 015, 1530, 3060 minutes) to group similar trips.
  • Aggregating data by key attributes such as user type, ride type, and time intervals to reduce file size and improve processing speed.

The resulting aggregated dataset provides a comprehensive yet compact view of usage patterns suitable for exploratory analysis in R Markdown.

Descriptive Analysis

The descriptive phase explores basic usage patterns and differences between members and casual riders. #### A. Total Rides by Member Type

This visualization reveals the overall ride volume distribution between user types.
Members dominate total ridership, indicating consistent usage likely for daily commuting or short trips.
Casual riders, while fewer, represent significant activity that could be converted into memberships with the right incentives.

data %>%
  group_by(member_type) %>%
  summarise(total_rides = sum(total_rides)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = member_type, y = total_rides, fill = member_type)) +
  geom_bar(stat = "identity") + scale_y_continuous(labels = comma) +
  labs(title = "Total Rides by Member Type", x = "User Type", y = "Number of Rides") +
  theme_minimal()

B. Average Ride Length by Member Type

This plot shows that casual users spend more time per ride than members as total rides for members are more than casual users but the average ride duration for both are almost similar.
While members focus on short, frequent rides, casual users tend to engage in longer, leisurely rides possibly during weekends or social outings.
This insight is key for designing targeted promotional strategies.

data %>%
  group_by(member_type) %>%
  summarise(avg_length = mean(avg_ride_length)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = member_type, y = avg_length, fill = member_type)) +
  geom_col() +
  labs(title = "Average Ride Length by Member Type", x = "User Type", y = "Average Minutes") +
  theme_minimal()

Time-Based Trends

A. Rides by Day of Week

Weekday vs.weekend analysis highlights behavioral contrasts: - Members have higher ridership on weekdays, suggesting routine or commute-based usage. - Casual riders peak on weekends, reflecting recreational patterns.

This clear separation reinforces the notion that Cyclistics user base splits between commuters and leisure users.

data %>%
  group_by(member_type, day_of_week) %>%
  summarise(total_rides = sum(total_rides)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = fct_relevel(day_of_week, c("Sunday","Monday","Tuesday","Wednesday","Thursday","Friday","Saturday")),
             y = total_rides, fill = member_type)) +
  geom_col(position = "dodge") + scale_y_continuous(labels = comma) +
  labs(title = "Rides by Day of Week", x = "Day", y = "Number of Rides") +
  theme_minimal()

Seasonal Trends

This visualization explores ridership patterns across months.
Both user types show strong seasonality, with ride counts peaking during summer months (JuneAugust) and dropping significantly in winter.
Casual riders display more pronounced seasonality reflecting fair-weather leisure rides while members maintain steadier engagement year-round.

Understanding these trends helps Cyclistic plan maintenance, marketing campaigns, and membership drives in alignment with seasonal fluctuations.

data %>%
  group_by(member_type, month) %>%
  summarise(total_rides = sum(total_rides)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = month, y = total_rides, group = member_type, color = member_type)) +

Insights and Discussion

  1. Rider Composition and Behavior
    Members account for the majority of total rides, reflecting established, habitual use likely for daily commuting or consistent travel needs.
    Casual users, though smaller in number, take longer rides, implying leisure or tourism-driven activity.

  2. Temporal Usage Patterns
    Members weekday peaks at 8 AM and 6 PM clearly align with work commute schedules.
    Casual riders dominate weekend and midday activity, often riding during daylight and warmer hours.

  3. Seasonal Variation
    Both groups ride more in summer, but casual riders show a steeper seasonal curve.
    Winter months present opportunities for member retention strategies and off-season engagement offers.

Overall Interpretation:
Cyclistics dual audience reflects two distinct behavioral groups: - Members = Consistency & Commuting - Casuals = Leisure & Exploration

This segmentation underscores the importance of customized marketing and operational strategies to drive membership growth and retention.

Recommendations

  1. Membership Conversion Campaigns:
    Incentivize frequent casual riders through discounted first-month memberships or ride bundles.

  2. Weekend and Tourist Promotions:
    Launch limited-time passes and partnerships with tourist destinations to attract leisure users.

  3. Operational Adjustments:
    Optimize bike distribution to align with morning/evening peaks for members and midday peaks for casual users.

  4. Seasonal Marketing:
    Focus advertising in spring and early summer when casual ridership begins to rise.

By tailoring its strategy to these insights, Cyclistic can increase overall engagement and encourage casual riders to become long-term members.
